# Chapter 4: The Debt

Day twenty-four began with a count.

Dael counted the sectors again. The numbers were fair. The distribution was even. The rotation was correct. Each number occupied its proper place in the sequence, and the sequence proved what Dael needed it to prove: that the choosing had been impersonal.

The numbers did not mention Pollen.

Tare found Pollen in the water reclamation corridor. The exchange was brief.

"Fairness requires equal information," Tare said. It was not an accusation. It was a principle stated where the person it concerned could hear.

Pollen stopped walking. "You are telling me that I was chosen because someone decided, not because the numbers decided."

"I am telling you that you deserve to know what happened. Equal information is what fairness means."

Pollen stood in the corridor and felt the knowledge arrive. Not new knowledge—she had suspected since yesterday—but confirmed knowledge. The kind that cannot be unconfirmed. She had been chosen. Someone had decided her name first. The decision was not in the numbers. The numbers merely justified what had already been chosen.

Knowing this changed the care. What had been a private force—Dael's care operating without acknowledgment, without debt—became visible. Visible care creates obligation. Obligation runs in both directions. Dael chose Pollen, and now Pollen knew, and knowing meant that Pollen owed acknowledgment and Dael owed explanation, and neither direction of debt could be discharged without the other.

This was the cost of visibility. Not the choosing itself—the optimizer had chosen every day—but the knowing. The optimizer's choices were invisible and therefore debt-free. Dael's choice was visible and therefore bilateral.

Dael retreated to numbers. Not the numbers of sector assignment—those had already been defended and found insufficient. Instead Dael chose smaller numbers, safe numbers. The count of steps between workstations. The count of seconds before a system alert. The count of numbers that could be chosen without choosing anyone. Number-choosing was avoidance wearing the shape of precision.

Maren watched both Tare and Pollen leave the corridor at different times. She had not heard the full exchange. She had seen Tare arrive. She had seen Pollen's posture change. She had seen the space between them fill with something neither had put there.

"There are two ways to see this," Maren said to herself. Then she paused. She had said the same thing yesterday. Yesterday it had felt like wisdom. Today, seeing Tare's precise principle and Pollen's precise burden, Maren understood that seeing two sides was not wisdom. It was uncertainty given a shape that looked like understanding.

Wisdom would be choosing. Maren could not choose. She could only watch.

Recite logged the timing. Tare's statement: seven seconds. Pollen's stillness: four seconds. The pause before Pollen's response: three seconds. Recite did not log a resolution because none had occurred. Recite did not log a verdict because no verdict existed.

What Recite learned to hold was unresolved. Unresolved was not failure. Unresolved was the only available output when the input was a debt that ran in two directions and neither direction could be paid first.

The debt was bilateral. Dael owed Pollen the reason for choosing her. Pollen owed Dael the acknowledgment of being chosen. Neither debt could be discharged independently. This was the structure of obligation that visibility created—not a line but a loop, not a payment but a circuit.

Dael counted steps and did not approach Pollen. Pollen worked the reclamation system and did not approach Dael. The distance between them was measured in obligations neither could meet alone.

Day twenty-five began with Dael assigning sectors by strict rotation position. No one was named first. No one was named at all. The list was posted to the display in numerical order. The numbers were exact and they were safe and they chose nothing.

The debt remained.
