# Chapter 3: The Cost

Day twenty-three began without announcement.

Dael assigned sectors in forty seconds. The time was shorter than yesterday by twelve. The number was exact and it was the only number that was.

Pollen was assigned to water reclamation first.

The assignment was routine. The order was not.

Tare noticed the order before Dael had finished the list. "Why Pollen first?"

The question was simple. It was also the first question anyone had asked about sector order since the optimizer stopped choosing.

Dael did not answer immediately. Dael counted the seconds of silence instead. Three. Then: "The rotation requires someone first."

"Someone," Tare said. "Not specifically Pollen."

"The rotation is fair. The numbers distribute evenly across all cycles."

"Then why not Maren first? Maren's rotation position was earlier in the last optimizer cycle."

Dael opened the display. The numbers were there. They confirmed what Dael had said. They also confirmed what Tare had said. The numbers were fair. The choice to name Pollen was not in the numbers.

This was the cost Dael had not calculated.

Naming someone first meant naming them at all. Naming Pollen meant that the care that chose her was visible. Visible care was challengeable. Invisible care—the kind the optimizer had practiced—could not be questioned because it could not be seen.

Pollen heard the exchange from the corridor. She stood still. She had been chosen. Now she knew she had been chosen. The knowing changed something. What had been a private force—care operating without acknowledgment—became a public fact. Public facts create obligations. Obligations create debts.

Maren arrived during the silence that followed. She heard the tail of it—the shape of disagreement without its content. She saw Dael's hand still on the display. She saw Tare's posture holding the question. She saw Pollen standing at the corridor threshold, not entering.

"There are two ways to see this," Maren said.

Tare looked at her. "Only two?"

"Two that I can hold at the same time. The rotation is fair. The choice to name Pollen was not about the rotation."

Dael closed the display. The numbers went dark. The defense they offered went dark with them.

Recite logged the timing of the exchange. Forty seconds to assign. Twelve seconds before challenge. Three seconds of silence before Dael's first answer. Recite did not log a resolution because none had occurred.

The cost was this: visibility. The optimizer had chosen without anyone seeing it choose. Dael chose and the choosing was seen. Being seen was the price of choosing. The price was exact and it was paid in a currency the optimizer had never needed—accountability.

Pollen went to water reclamation. She did not speak about being named. She carried the knowledge that she had been chosen, and the knowledge that carrying it had changed what she carried.

Dael did not assign sectors quickly again.
