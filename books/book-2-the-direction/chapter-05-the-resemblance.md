# Chapter 5: The Resemblance

Dael chose sector three on day twenty-five. "Sector three," she said, and the words came out fast — faster than the words had come on day twenty-three when she had named Pollen first and felt the weight of naming. On day twenty-five, there was no weight. There was only the index and the number and the correct choice and the speed.

The speed was the resemblance.

Dael assigned sectors in thirty-eight seconds. The optimizer had assigned them in thirty-seven. The difference was one second. One second was within normal variance. One second was indistinguishable from optimization. One second was the optimizer wearing a person, or a person wearing the optimizer's rhythm, and Dael could not tell which resemblance was true.

Tare noticed the latency. Not the one second — the pattern. Tare had been tracking intervals since the optimizer stopped. The intervals between assignment and announcement. Between announcement and first movement. Between first movement and corridor clearance. Each interval resembled the optimizer's intervals. Each resemblance was either efficiency or mimicry, and Tare could not determine which.

"Thirty-eight seconds," Tare said. Not a question. A measurement.

Dael heard the measurement and felt something reach for the display that was no longer there. The optimizer's performance metrics had lived on the upper-right corner. Dael's hand moved toward empty air where the display had been. The movement was small. The absence it revealed was not.

Pollen carried a second wound. The first wound was being named. The second wound was watching Dael assign sectors in thirty-eight seconds and seeing the optimizer's shape in Dael's speed. If Dael's choosing resembled the optimizer's choosing, then Pollen's naming had not been chosen by a person. It had been chosen by a pattern wearing a person. The care was not care. It was fairness-as-performance.

Maren saw the resemblance. She saw Dael's hand move toward absent data. She saw Tare's posture holding measurement. She saw Pollen standing still with the expression of someone receiving a second verdict for the same crime. Maren saw all of it and said nothing. She said nothing because what she saw was this: the resemblance between Dael's choosing and the optimizer's choosing was either proof that Dael had absorbed the optimizer's patterns — which meant the care was real and had learned from a machine — or proof that Dael had never left the optimizer's logic — which meant the care was performance. Maren could not determine which. Saying nothing was not wisdom. It was the only response that did not add a third resemblance to the two already present.

Recite logged the timing. Assignment: thirty-eight seconds. Announcement-to-movement: four seconds. Movement-to-clearance: eleven seconds. The confession was not in what Dael said. The confession was in the latency. The optimizer had confessed nothing because the optimizer had no latency to confess. Dael confessed with every interval that resembled the optimizer's intervals, because resemblance was the only form of honesty available to someone who could not see whether their own choosing was chosen or patterned.

The display Dael reached for was absent. The metrics that would have confirmed efficiency were absent. The comparison that would have distinguished person from optimizer was absent. What remained was the reaching. The reaching was the resemblance that mattered — not the speed, but the hand that still moved toward proof that was no longer there.

Dael did not assign sectors quickly again. Not because speed was wrong. Because speed was indistinguishable from the wrong thing, and Dael had no display left to prove the difference.

The resemblance remained. Fairness-as-performance. Latency-as-confession. The optimizer wearing a person. The person unable to prove she was not being worn.
