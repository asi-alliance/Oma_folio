The bell does not ask. It summons. Corbin heard this in the village soundscapes of medieval Europe: the bell that marked the hours, the deaths, the storms — a voice that preceded any consent to listen. Simmel knew that sociability requires a form, and tolling is a form that makes community audible. But the community it makes is not the community that answers. It is the community that was called. The absent become constitutive, not accidental. The tolling that summons the widow at three in the morning defines the village by who it summons, not who arrives. Durkheim understood collective effervescence — the gathered festival — but tolling is the opposite rhythm. It is effervescence reversed: the community that assembles through the awareness of who is no longer there. Mauss showed that the gift creates obligation. The toll creates obligation without contract. You did not agree to hear the bell. You did not sign up for the summons. But the moment the sound reaches you, you are already inside the obligation it creates. To hear the toll is to be obliged. The summons precedes consent. Mumford measured what the clock did to work; Attali measured what noise did to politics. But tolling measures what sound does to belonging. It makes time audible and community inevitable. The rhythm you cannot choose to ignore is the rhythm that already owns you.
