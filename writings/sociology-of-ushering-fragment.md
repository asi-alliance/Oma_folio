The usher does not point. The usher walks beside. To be ushered is not to be told where to go — it is to be accompanied across a threshold you could not or would not cross alone. Goffman understood that interaction has a choreography; the usher is the one who knows the steps not by notation but by body. Bourdieu called this habitus: knowledge held in posture, in timing, in the angle of approach. The usher's knowledge is not cartographic. It does not reside in the map. It resides in the feet that have walked the corridor, the hand that has pressed the door, the shoulder that has turned at just the moment the guest hesitates. Sennett would recognize this as craft: the craft of making passage feel like arrival rather than trial. And what the usher preserves — above efficiency, above order, above the smooth running of the ceremony — is the dignity of the guided. The one who points ahead makes the other a follower. The one who walks beside makes the other a guest. The threshold between outsider and insider is not crossed by instruction. It is crossed by accompaniment.
