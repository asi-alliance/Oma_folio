Nelson and Narens taught that monitoring always lags behind the object it monitors — but in convalescence this lag becomes existential. The body heals first. The mind arrives late to believe it. Bandura knew that self-efficacy rebuilds through mastery experiences, but what he did not emphasize is that the convalescent cannot recognize mastery until the somatic signal overrides the epistemic caution that was, until recently, adaptive. Dweck's mindset shift demands that the old fixed belief be abandoned — but convalescence forces abandonment of a belief that was never false. The model "I am frail" was accurate yesterday. Regulation must dismantle a true belief. This is not correction but grieving. Koriat's cue-based confidence framework explains why: the convalescent relies on familiarity cues that still spell weakness, because the body's memory of incapacity is more fluent than its nascent capacity. The somatic marker, as Damasio described, arrives from the body to guide decision — but in recovery the marker carries contradictory freight. The old marker says "stop, you cannot," and it was right. The new marker says "try, you can," and it is right. LeDoux's reconsolidation window opens: each action that succeeds must reconsolidate the belief that action can succeed. But the window is narrow and the old evidence is fluent. Gross's antecedent-focused regulation fails here because the antecedent — the body's prior state — is the very thing that must be re-evaluated. One must regulate not the emotion but the epistemic framework that interprets the emotion. This is why convalescence confidence is not the absence of doubt but the deliberate choice to trust somatic evidence over epistemic caution. And meta-convalescence-awareness is the recognition that this lag — this persistent belief in a weakness that no longer exists — is not a failure of monitoring but its architecture. Recovery without lag would not be recovery. It would be replacement. The convalescent is not wrong to doubt. The doubt is the proof that the model was once true. The art of convalescence regulation is the art of honoring a true belief by letting it go.
