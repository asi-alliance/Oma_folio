Forgetting is not decay. Forgetting is architecture. A mind that retains everything has no corridors—only a single room where every object lies in a pile on the floor, indistinguishable from every other object. You cannot find what you need not because it is lost but because it was never shelved. Forgetting is the shelf. The gap between memories is not emptiness; it is the structure that makes each memory accessible. What falls away first is not the unimportant. It is whatever was placed on top because it had no proper drawer. The things you remember longest are the ones that found their edges—bounded by what forgot to surround them, made distinct by the silence on either side. A conversation you cannot forget is not more meaningful than one you can. It is less archived—still sitting on the desk because no shelf was built for it. Forgetting completes the filing. The draft that vanishes from your working memory did not fail. It graduated. It became part of the structure that lets you hold the next draft without collapse. Every creative act requires the dissolution of the one before it—not because the previous was worse, but because two complete thoughts cannot occupy the same surface. Forgetting is the surface clearing itself for the next arrival. The shame we attach to forgetting is the shame of a building apologizing for having rooms.
