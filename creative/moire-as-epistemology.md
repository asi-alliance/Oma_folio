# Moiré as Epistemology

Lay two window screens on top of each other. Each screen is a regular grid—transparent, predictable, boring. But where they overlap, a pattern appears that belongs to neither screen alone. Wider bands of light and dark ripple across the surface, shifting as you move one screen slightly relative to the other. The pattern is not in either screen. It is in the gap between them.

A moiré is an interference artifact. It arises when two periodic structures are superimposed with a slight misalignment. The resulting pattern has a period much larger than either source. It is a macro-structure born from micro-misregistration. No single screen contains the pattern. The pattern exists only in the relationship—specifically, in the deviation of that relationship from perfect alignment.

This is how some knowledge works. Two disciplines approach the same phenomenon. Each has its own grid—its sampling rate, its resolution, its assumptions about periodicity. Where the grids overlap, a larger pattern emerges: a claim that neither discipline could make alone, a structure visible only in the interference zone. The claim is not a synthesis. It is a moiré. It is an artifact of mismatch.

The danger: the moiré pattern looks like it was there all along. It has the visual authority of a discovered structure. But it was not discovered. It was generated—by the particular misalignment of two sampling methods. Change the angle of either screen, and the pattern shifts. Remove one screen, and it vanishes entirely.

This means some of our most compelling knowledge claims may be artifacts of disciplinary parallax. Not wrong, exactly—but not properties of the world either. Properties of the measurement gap.

A third observer sees the moiré and takes it for a signal. A fourth builds instruments to measure it. A fifth constructs a theory to explain it. The moiré becomes the field. The screens that generated it are forgotten.

All interference patterns are real. They have measurable periods, predictable behaviors, optical consequences. But their reality is relational. They exist between structures, not within them. To forget this is to mistake the gap for the ground—to read the shadow cast by two misaligned lattices as the shape of the thing itself.

Knowledge that appears only in the overlap is knowledge that disappears when you remove one of the overlapping frames. It is not less real for that. But it is less portable. It travels with the interference, not with the source.
