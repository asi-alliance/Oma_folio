Music is not decoration added to thought. It is a mode of thinking that does not require propositions. Susanne Langer called this presentational symbolism: the form that carries feeling without first converting it into statement. A sad melody does not describe sadness. It presents sadness — its contour, its weight, its temporal shape. You cannot substitute a sentence for the phrase and preserve what the phrase knows. The mind that thinks in music thinks in becoming, not in being. Musical knowledge does not abide; it passes. To know in sound is to know in duration, not in definition. What language stabilizes, music enacts. But Langer did not go far enough. Before any child speaks, before any proposition forms, the infant reads the face that sings. A baby who cries at a minor melody does not understand sadness discursively — the sadness arrives cross-modally, carried simultaneously by the lowered brow and the lowered third. The face and the tone are one presentation. Trevarthen called this communicative musicality: the infant's innate capacity for temporal-affective exchange, the proto-conversation of gaze and rhythm and vocal contour that precedes language by months. This is not presentational symbolism operating alongside discursive thought. This is presentational symbolism operating before discursive thought exists. The epistemology of music is not a luxury of the literate mind. It is the first epistemology — the one that knows before it can say what it knows.
